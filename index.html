1.1.1.1. Introduction to SEAL

Welcome to the Sensors, Energy, and Automation Laboratory (SEAL)! UW SEAL  provides ample opportunities and resources to acquire work experience and letters of recommendation, to establish connections with alumni, and to grow your social network. Once you become a successful associate, you might transfer to a paid position, launch a startup, get into a top graduate school, or pursue a modeling career - SEAL has many scenarios. Our alumni have become professors, technology millionaires, patent attorneys, medical doctors, military leaders, and the list goes on.

No matter where you are located, you will have access to tools and opportunities to foster your academic and professional growth. In return, you need to display independence, initiative, and perseverance. You are expected to go through the training materials, find projects of interest and reach out to project leaders for roles on your own. We have multiple modes to facilitate virtual collaboration (Slack, Zoom), so be sure to utilize them to stay connected with the lab.

SEAL has over one hundred participants. We have students from engineering, computer science, mechanical engineering, business, graphics design, arts, English majors, biology, and more. Not everyone is a student: SEAL also has advisors - professors, industry leaders, and recent alumni. SEAL members' ages vary from 16 years old (high school interns) to over 70 years old ( Ex- Associates).

A word of caution .  Several times, we ask you to stop and think - are you ready for this? As a starting Associate, you will be expected to spend at least 10 hours per week working on lab tasks. Many students oversubscribe to courses, low-grade jobs, or hobbies and fail to commit enough time. It is a very frequent situation. When you do not carry your weight, it negatively affects other team members. If you find yourself in this situation - please inform your team leader and take a break from the lab - or leave permanently, but on good terms. You can return to the lab when and if you have enough time for this endeavor. If you just quit paying attention to your share of tasks, letting down your team, you would still have to leave the lab, but then you will not be invited back.

A word of encouragement.  You don’t have to have experience. This lab consists of students who are constantly trying new things. We have training sequences, mentorship, advising, and group support. Prior knowledge is not an important prerequisite for being successful in SEAL - but consistent effort is.

1.1.1.2. Your SEAL Career Path

Participating in SEAL will provide you a structured experience in research, technical communication, and project management. Students are expected to take initiative and manage tasks independently, contributing to a collaborative effort. As such, the lab offers a variety of opportunities to build relevant skills, progress your academic career, and pursue unique professional paths. Below is a generalized list of opportunities SEAL provides, however this list is ever growing and will change depending on the opportunities available and the needs of students.

Career and Professional Development Opportunities

  • Strong Resume Building: Students are expected to regularly reflect on and update their resumes with tangible achievements like projects, leadership roles, technical deliverables, and awards.
  • Letters of Recommendation: Eligible students who perform well and avoid stale tasks can request strong letters of recommendation for graduate school, scholarships, and jobs.
  • Internship and Scholarship Access:  The lab encourages and facilitates participation in scholarships, competitions, and internships—often leveraging its alumni network.
  • Paid Positions:  Students can transition into paid roles within the lab by contributing to successful "quests" (projects or deliverables).
  • Industry and Alumni Connections:  SEAL provides exposure to industry mentors and alumni in roles such as professors, entrepreneurs, patent attorneys, and more.

Academic Enrichment and Graduate School Preparation

  • Research Credit:  Students can receive university research or course credit for their time in SEAL.
  • Graduate School Preparation: The program includes mentoring on applying to and succeeding in graduate school, including how to travel to conferences and present work.
  • Publication and Proposal Experience:  Opportunities to write and co-author journal articles, conference papers, and grant proposals (e.g., SBIR/STTR).

Skill Development

  • Technical Skills: Hands-on experience in:
  • Energy and sustainability projects
  • High-voltage safety
  • Google Sheets, Kanban, EndNote, coding tools
  • Lab management tools like dashboards and Quest/YBR systems
  • Writing and Communication:  Formal training in technical writing, documentation, and creating research posters and presentations.
  • Leadership and Project Management:
  • Leading groups and assigning tasks
  • Conflict resolution and managing underperforming team members
  • Organizing meetings and contributing to lab infrastructure

Entrepreneurial and Innovation Exposure

  • Startup Opportunities:  SEAL encourages and mentors entrepreneurial ventures.
  • Business Plan Writing: Students learn how to write executive summaries and business plans.
  • Innovation-Focused Culture: The lab's philosophy promotes independent thinking and initiative-driven participation—skills valuable for launching new ventures.

Recognition and Certification

  • SEAL Ranking System:  Students accumulate points across five areas (citizenship, leadership, certification, deliverables, academic development) to rise in rank.
  • SEAL Certificate of Achievement:  A formal certificate recognizing completion of the training program and mastery of key skillsets.

1.1.1.3. The “We Eat What We Catch” Principle

Our lab is set in an academic institution. As such,  the lab itself does not sell products or generate revenue. Instead, we follow the principle of “we eat what we catch.” Students are expected to pursue and contribute to successful projects, proposals, publications, and other deliverables—these are the “catches” that lead to funding, recognition, and advancement. Unlike traditional environments that guarantee steady outcomes, SEAL operates more like a hunting model, where success depends on initiative, effort, and risk-taking. While not all pursuits will succeed, students who engage consistently, contribute meaningfully, and take ownership of their work are more likely to access paid positions, recommendation letters, and career-building experiences.

        You may think of the farming approach as a traditional route, where plentiful crops and livestock slowly grow bigger over long stretches of time - it’s a reliable operation, similar to a corporation. This is akin to getting a job in a large tech company like Microsoft. We use the hunt approach note because we like it, but it’s just how the academic world is made. Students who are self-driven find powerful projects that match their skill sets, check slack channels, and pick areas of work for projects that vary in time frame (e.g., weeks, months, years).

In summary, SEAL uses the 'hunt' approach, so that anyone that is motivated, self-driven and hardworking, has many opportunities to find their own successes, learn from their own failures, and increase their own knowledge in the field. The lab will reward and enable passionate and driven students who proactively tackle new challenges.

1.1.1.4. Growth Pathways in SEAL

Joining SEAL is not just about completing assignments. It’s about unlocking a pathway of professional growth, mentorship, and opportunity. Every Associate begins with foundational responsibilities, but with consistent effort and initiative, those same responsibilities can evolve into leadership roles, funded positions, and even entrepreneurial ventures.

All new Associates share the same core expectations — these are the habits that form the foundation of your SEAL journey:

  • Dedicate at least 10 hours per week  to lab tasks.
  • Stay consistently active  in meetings, Slack, and SEAL systems.
  • Use SEAL Life tools  to monitor your progress and maintain good standing.
  • Take initiative — seek out projects  and opportunities proactively.
  • Communicate clearly: write concise, complete updates using proper grammar.
  • Solve problems independently  — confusion is never a reason for inaction.
  • Contribute to lab efforts that lead to tangible results — our “catches.”

        At first, your assignments may seem simple or repetitive. This early stage helps you learn the system, understand expectations, and demonstrate reliability. Leaders observe your initiative and follow-through; proving your consistency and work ethic  is the key that unlocks more ambitious tasks and leadership opportunities.

As you progress, the scope and impact of your work expand dramatically. Students who excel find themselves leading teams, managing projects, writing proposals, publishing research, and developing new technologies.

Typical advancement pathways include:

  • Course Credit (optional)  – for academic recognition of research effort.
  • Research Experience & Training  – guided technical or writing mentorship.
  • Flexible Schedules  – design your own research routine around your studies.
  • Mentorship  – direct access to senior students, alumni, and faculty.
  • Telecommuting Options  – contribute from anywhere in the world.
  • Publications & Proposals  – co-author journal papers and grant submissions.
  • Scholarships & Awards  – leverage SEAL projects for academic funding.
  • Entrepreneurial Pathways  – work on technologies with commercialization potential through small business partnerships.

The bottom line is: the longer you stay active, the more SEAL  invests in you  — through training, mentorship, and exposure to professional-grade research experiences. SEAL operates on the principle of “We Eat What We Catch.” While new students typically begin as volunteers, every Associate has the opportunity to earn funding  through results-driven contributions. Paid positions are awarded to Associates who demonstrate reliability, initiative, and measurable impact.

There are several ways to achieve compensation:

  • Contributing to successful proposals or funded projects.
  • Building a strong record of deliverables reflected in your YBR Gold  and SEAL Rank .
  • Participating in Prize Pool initiatives , where Associates submit focused project proposals that may be prioritized for lab funding.
  • Excelling within high-performing groups and teams whose Quests achieve measurable success.

Remember: success in SEAL is self-propelled . The harder and smarter you work, the faster you rise — from trainee to contributor, to leader, to funded researcher. Your growth is tracked through both quantitative and qualitative measures. High-performing Associates become eligible for recommendation letters, travel opportunities, leadership nominations, and project funding.

Strong performance means:

  • Staying active in your group and keeping tasks current.
  • Updating your progress transparently.
  • Contributing reliably to collective outcomes.

Success at SEAL is not about perfection — it’s about ownership, perseverance, and steady progress.  When one Associate takes responsibility, the entire lab benefits.

At SEAL, growth is earned through initiative and sustained effort. You start by learning the systems and expectations, but you can end up publishing papers, leading proposals, or even launching your own company.

Your journey begins the moment you decide to take ownership of it.

1.1.2. Understanding SEAL Life

1.1.2.1. Glossary of SEAL Terms

Associates:  All active members or SEAL who are held accountable for their performance. Associates have to maintain Good standing in SEAL Clan Life.

Alumni:  Lab members who left the lab in Good status after being in the lab for two quarters or more.

Ex- Associates:  Associates who did not make enough contributions to be Alumni, but exited the lab with 1 or more lives. Could come back with permission.  

Ex-Communicado :  Associates who left in GameOver status. Not invited back to SEAL.

Command Center:  The Command Center is a gateway to all active projects at SEAL. Once you have a project, you can use this gateway to access all of your group materials, including quests, reports, kanban boards, documents (& manuscripts), and other storage files.

Dashboard:  Dashboard is your road to success. It is a tool that displays your stats, your Quest Portfolio, and the details of your SEAL Rank.

Control Panel: This spreadsheet contains the quests, TPS, kanban, Overview, and Docs tabs of certain teams. Accessible from the command center.

Group Leader: This person will be your main source of contact for tasks and inquiries related to your. team. They are the senior most members and lead the entire group by assigning tasks.

Team Leader:  SEAL groups consist of Teams. Team leader is one level down from Group leader in the executive ladder.

KSS (Kanban Super Sheet):  A board used to assign tasks to people and coordinate group workflow.

Quests:  The “Quests” tool is used to keep track of all tasks across many different Kanbans. Quests have color-coded cells to help people quickly understand their task situation. Each group has its own “Quests” sheet. This link can be found on the Command Center.

Tasks: Tasks are things that need to be done. Tasks are assigned in two ways: 1) properly added onto a Kanban (e.g., your group kanban, global kanban), or 2) given via word of mouth (e.g., over Slack, during a Zoom meeting, over the phone). You should add tasks that are assigned via word-of-mouth onto a suitable Kanban immediately so that you do not forget about them.

SEAL Clan Life: This spreadsheet is designed to be your “one-stop-shop” for accessing different resources at SEAL.

YBR (Yellow Brick Road):  A workplace management tool designed to keep associates on track with their tasks within a specific project.

YBR Delta: Difference between the current YBR score and the YBR score seven days ago. If YBR Delta is zero, it means that an Associate is not active in quests. A zero score is unacceptable. A high score tends to be correlated with high performance, but it is not always the case.

1.1.2.2. Core SEAL Tools and Systems

SEAL operates through an interconnected network of Google-based tools. Together, these systems allow Associates to manage their work, track progress, and stay aligned with the lab’s goals. Every Associate interacts with several of these tools regularly, and understanding how they fit together is essential to thriving in SEAL.

SEAL Clan Life:

Purpose:  The central hub for the entire lab. Every Associate, Affiliate, and Ex-Associate is listed here, with key information such as group, team, SEAL rank, and activity status.

Your row in SEAL Clan Life reflects your current standing — both symbolically and operationally. It connects you to the rest of SEAL’s digital infrastructure, including your dashboard, your quests, and your group’s tools.

  • Think of it as your home base  in SEAL.
  • It links to everything else you will use.
  • Staying accurate and up to date here ensures that automated systems and leadership dashboards can properly reflect your progress.

Failure to maintain your Clan Life row can result in triggering an range of automated “non-compliance” notes in the lab system, which can affect your standing or access until corrected.

Groups and Teams:

SEAL is organized into several Groups , each focused on a broad research or operational area (for example, Plasma, ITAC, Embedded, BizTech, or Sudoku). Within each Group are smaller Teams  that specialize in particular projects or technologies.

  • Groups  act like research departments.
  • Teams  are smaller, focused units within those Groups that handle specific deliverables or technical goals.

Your Group and Team determine what kinds of tasks, meetings, and quests you’ll be involved in. During onboarding, you’ll learn how to identify and join them through SEAL Clan Life and the Command Center.

Dashboards:

Every Associate has a personal Dashboard , which functions as your mission control for SEAL work. It automatically pulls data from the Quests and Kanbans that you are assigned to, helping you track your current tasks, performance metrics, and upcoming deadlines.

From your Dashboard, you can:

  • View your assigned tasks across all Quests.
  • Update task statuses (e.g., To Do, Doing, Review).
  • Monitor your YBR progress and total Gold.
  • See your weekly engagement indicators and activity scores.

Dashboards make it possible to stay organized across multiple projects without having to open each group sheet individually.

Quests:

Quests  are at the heart of SEAL’s project management system. Each Quest is a separate spreadsheet dedicated to a specific project, proposal, paper, or deliverable. Within each Quest are task tracking tools that record progress, responsibilities, and completion metrics.

Key points:

  • Tasks are always entered into the system  — never left in chat or notes.
  • If someone assigns you a task verbally or through Slack, make sure it’s logged on the appropriate Quest sheet.
  • You will not receive tasks until you’ve officially joined a Quest.

How These Systems Work Together:

These tools are deeply connected:

  • SEAL Clan Life  → lists every Associate, linking you to your Dashboard  and Group resources .
  • Dashboards  → pull live task data from Quests  and help you stay on top of your deliverables.
  • Quests  → exist within each Group  or Team , and the results feed back into your SEAL Rank and performance metrics.
  • Groups/Teams  → provide leadership, mentorship, and context for your projects.

When you use them together, these tools form a seamless digital ecosystem — one that replaces traditional office management with transparent, data-driven collaboration.

Understanding how SEAL’s systems fit together will save you countless hours and help you make a strong first impression. Your Clan Life row  is your identity, your Dashboard  is your cockpit, your Group and Team  define your environment, and your Quests  are where you achieve results. Together, they are what make SEAL a fully digital, self-organizing laboratory.

1.1.2.3. SEAL Ranks and Privileges

The SEAL Ranking System is how we recognize and reward growth, leadership, and consistent contribution. Your rank reflects not just how much you’ve done, but how reliably and independently you’ve supported the lab’s mission. As you take initiative, complete projects, and help others succeed, your rank naturally rises, and so do your opportunities.

Every Associate in SEAL has a numerical Rank , shown in SEAL Clan Life (Column C) . This rank is calculated automatically from your accumulated points across several key dimensions — such as deliverables, leadership, citizenship, and academic development.

Ranks grow as you:

  • Complete quests and tasks on time.
  • Contribute to major deliverables such as proposals, publications, or patents.
  • Mentor others or take on leadership roles.
  • Engage regularly in lab events and maintain strong communication.

You can view the detailed breakdown of how ranks are calculated here: [LINK]

Your SEAL Rank serves as a transparent, data-driven reflection of your professional maturity inside the lab. Higher ranks demonstrate mastery, initiative, and reliability — qualities that open the door to advanced opportunities.

With higher rank come greater privileges — both in terms of what you can access and how much the lab invests in you. While every Associate is encouraged to participate in projects, higher-ranking members are often recommended for leadership roles, funding opportunities, and external representation.

Typical privileges associated with strong SEAL performance include:

  • Eligibility for Recommendation Letters  – based on consistency and accountability.
  • Priority for Paid Positions and Stipends  – offered to members who maintain strong, reliable performance.
  • Conference or Travel Support  – for those actively contributing to funded projects.
  • Mentorship Roles  – opportunities to guide new Associates and manage team quests.
  • Recognition and Awards  – through SEAL events or special internal nominations.

It’s important to note that privileges follow performance, not tenure . What matters most is consistent, high-quality work — regardless of when you joined SEAL.

Everyone at SEAL has the potential to earn funding, but doing so requires initiative. One common way to qualify is through the SEAL Prize Pool , where Associates propose and “place bets” on new ideas, projects, or technologies that could advance the lab’s goals. Funding is awarded to those who submit focused, high-value proposals — the kind that demonstrate ownership, collaboration, and measurable outcomes.

Paid positions are not assigned automatically; they are earned through visible results, strong communication, and reliability. System metrics like SEAL Rank  and YBR Gold  often reflect  that success, but the deciding factor is your track record of steady, dependable work.

SEAL’s privileges come with shared accountability. Associates are expected to uphold high professional standards, not just individually but as members of their groups. When a group allows its tasks to go stale or its deliverables to fall behind, it affects everyone’s standing.

  • Groups with Stale Tasks  (visible in Command Center → Column E ) lose recommendation eligibility until those tasks are resolved.
  • A score below 100  means some tasks have gone inactive and must be addressed before recommendation or advancement requests can be processed.

This isn’t about punishment — it’s about ensuring every Associate learns to manage responsibility collaboratively. When one person strengthens their work habits, the entire lab becomes more capable and trusted.

Your SEAL Rank is a mirror of your reliability, initiative, and contribution to the lab’s success. As you climb, new opportunities will unfold: leadership roles, funded projects, professional mentorship, and letters of recommendation.

But above all, SEAL rewards those who take ownership — not just of their tasks, but of their growth. Consistent, reliable effort is the currency of opportunity in SEAL.

1.1.2.4. Group Meetings and Quest Sessions

SEAL’s main coordination rhythm revolves around two recurring meetings — Group Meetings and Quest Sessions. Both are scheduled in the Scheduler, accessible through the Command Center.

Associates are expected to fill out their Group’s “Weekly Report” every two weeks before their Group’s weekly meeting, and provide small updates to their existing weekly reports during the time in between. Column I (i) within the Associates sheet in SEAL Clan Life will provide a link to your Group’s weekly report.

To view upcoming meetings:

  1. Open the Scheduler  (linked in the Command Center).
  2. Click through the tabs to find your Group’s weekly meeting time  and lab-wide Quest Session slots.
  3. Use these times to plan your week and ensure attendance.

Group Meetings:

Each research group meets once per week for an hour and a half.

  • First 30 minutes:  Team-only coordination led by Group or Team Leads.
  • Second 30 minutes:  The Lab Director joins for broader discussion and updates.
  • Last 30 minutes: Time put aside for any immediate tasks.

These meetings ensure your team stays aligned and visible to leadership.

Quest Sessions:

Quest Sessions  are open lab-wide work sessions held on Discord. They’re not formal meetings — just times when Associates gather to work in real time, ask questions, and collaborate across groups.

  • You must attend at least one Quest Session per week.
  • Use these sessions to make progress on your tasks, or to ask for new ones if you’re looking for work.
  • The Lab Director or other leads may also join to assign or discuss live tasks.

Quest Sessions are SEAL’s open workspace — a chance to work together, get help, and stay connected to the rest of the lab.

In Summary:

  • Check the Scheduler  for both your Group Meeting  and Quest Session  times.
  • Group Meetings  = coordination and updates.
  • Quest Sessions  = flexible, live work time with the community.

1.2.2.2. Understanding Quests and Tasks

SEAL’s work is organized through an interconnected system of Quests, Kanbans, and Tasks. Together, they define what the lab is doing, who is doing it, and how progress is tracked.

Terminology:

  • Kanban: A digital board used to assign and track tasks. SEAL uses customized Kanbans for each group and project, allowing leaders to delegate work clearly. You can learn more about Kanbans here.
  • Quest: Each Quest  represents a SEAL project — such as a paper, proposal, patent, or report. Every Quest has its own spreadsheet with links to related Kanbans, reports, and documents. Tasks for that Quest appear as color-coded cells under your name. You can find Quest links on your group’s Command Center  page. Learn more here.
  • Task: A Task  is a specific, finite action that needs to be completed.
    Tasks can be:
  • Added directly to a Kanban  (the proper method), or
  • Assigned verbally (via Slack, meetings, or calls) — in which case you must add them to the appropriate Kanban yourself.
  • Tasks are divided into two categories:
  • YBR Tasks  – standardized, recurring steps used in all Quests (e.g., completing proposal sections 0.1–4.4).
  • Kanban Tasks  – custom, project-specific actions defined by the person assigning them.

Understanding Your Quest Name

SEAL uses a standardized Quest naming convention so that anyone can quickly understand what a Quest is, how urgent it is, and where it belongs. This name is used for sorting, prioritizing, and locating Quests across systems (Quest sheets, YBRs, Kanbans, and the KSS).

  1. Usual format when naming a Quest/YBR: [Letter][#]-[Quest] [Direction] [Agency] [Type] [Codename] [Status] [Year]
  2. How to Read Each Part:
  1. [Letter] = Quest category/type
  1. A = Proposals
  2. B = Papers
  3. C = Courses
  4. G = Groups
  5. I = Patent (IP)
  6. P = Projects
  7. R = Reports (including IAC Reports)
  8. S = SEAL Certificates
  9. V = Variety (everything else)
  10. W = Websites
  11. Y = Achievement Quests
  12. Z = Frozen / To-Be-Archived Quests (kept at the bottom)
  1. [#] = Priority
  1. 1 = High priority
  2. 2 = Medium priority
  3. 3 = Low priority (slower pace)
  4. 4 = No priority / Frozen
  1. [Quest] = Quest subtype (what the deliverable is)
  1. Examples: Prop, Paper, Course, Project, Report, White Paper, etc.
  1. [Direction] = Research group
  1. Examples: Embedded, Plasma, ITAC
  1. [Agency] = Target organization
  1. Examples: NIH, IEEE, Amazon
  1. [Type] = Mechanism / RFP category
  1. Examples: STTR, Conference, Grant, Generic, R21, etc.
  1. [Codename] = Unique short identifier (1–2 words)
  1. Examples: Mask, Breathing, Avocado, Wearables
  1. [Status] = Submission state
  1. Examples: New, Resub, Revision, etc.
  1. [Year] = Expected delivery/submission year
  1. Quick Example
  1. A1. Prop-Plasma NIOSH R21 Mask New 2023 can be decoded as:
  1. A = Proposal
  2. 1 = High priority
  3. Prop = Proposal quest
  4. Plasma = Research group
  5. NIOSH = Target agency
  6. R21 = Mechanism/type
  7. Mask = Codename
  8. New = Status
  9. 2023 = Year
  1. Why This Matters
  1. It prevents confusion between similarly named projects.
  2. It makes it easy for leads to triage work and assign tasks.
  3. It helps you quickly confirm you’re opening/updating the correct Quest when you follow links from the Command Center or when you work across multiple Quests.

Completing your First Task:

When you finish this Training Program, you’ll be ready to start completing tasks within your project Quest. Follow these steps:

  1. Go to your project’s Quest sheet  (linked in the Command Center).
  2. Look for your name — if any cells under it are black, red, yellow, or teal , that means you have tasks assigned.
  3. Click the Kanban link in that cell to open the task board.
  4. On the Kanban, find your task, read the description, and set the status to “2. Doing.”
  5. Add regular updates in the “Obstacles”  column so your lead can follow your progress.
  6. When finished, write how you completed it, and change the status to “3. Review.”
  7. Your lead will review and then mark it as “4. Done.”  (Don’t change it yourself.)

If you have no assigned tasks, first ask your Quest Lead on the Quests you are on for tasks. If you aren’t on a Quest yet, ask your Group Lead which Quests need assistance right now.

1.2.2.3. Understanding the Weekly Rhythm

SEAL’s workflow is built around consistency. Each Associate is expected to dedicate about 10 hours per week to lab efforts — enough to make meaningful progress while maintaining balance with classes and other responsibilities.

Terminology:

  • Task-Specific Deadlines: Most small tasks do not have strict deadlines. It’s your responsibility to fit them within your weekly commitment window. Don’t sit on tasks too long — delays can hold back your entire project. Tasks marked 1-Critical under Priority  must be completed as soon as possible. If you find a task too difficult or time-consuming, inform your Group Lead immediately and post updates regularly.
  • Project-Wide Deadlines:  Instead of assigning every task a due date, SEAL uses broader deadlines — shared project deadlines that guide each group’s overall progress. When you fall behind or skip your weekly commitment, the whole team’s output suffers.
  • Time Commitment: You are expected to contribute at least 10 hours per week of honest, focused effort. If your availability changes, notify your Group Lead early so tasks can be reassigned or rescheduled. Use your Dashboard and view your tasks to monitor due dates, task priorities, and any warnings.

3.2.3. Recurring Tasks

Overview

  • On any Kanban, you will see a “recurring tasks” section. (See screenshot below)
  • Recurring tasks will automatically switch back to “To Do” status after a set period of time.
  • This enables you to temporarily mark a task as “Done”, and then, in a few days, it will switch back to “To Do”.

How does it work ?

  1. If there’s no text in the “Repeat?” column, it is not a recurring task.
  2. If the repeat column is set to “yes”, then the task will reawaken after a set period of time.
  3. The “how often” column determines how often the task should switch back to “1. To do”. See [ Link ] for image.

Please study the logic below :

  • If “How Often?” is set to “Every 2 days”, then it's set to turn red if the task hasn't been touched for 2 days.
  • If the task was last edited May 12th, and it is May 15th, then it indicates that the task has not been addressed for more than 3 days.
  • Thus, it is set back to “To Do”.
  • However, if anyone edits the task, then the “Last Modified” column would be May 15th.
  • Assuming today is May 15th
  • the task would be edited “less than 2 days ago”,
  • and the task would be set to whatever the “Manual Status” column says.
  • This is working as intended: Recurring tasks cannot be marked “Done” unless the task is edited more recently than the “How Often?” column states.

Please Study a Common Problem :

Some mark the recurring task of “Edit the manuscript once a day” as “Done”, without explaining how they edited the manuscript.

  1. The task will remain “To Do” even though the task was marked “Done”.
  1. What went wrong?  Because edits were not explained, the manuscript in the “Updates” column, which means the “Last Modified” column is out of date.

How do I fix this?

Simply type in your latest update into the “Updates / Obstacles” column, and the “Last Modified” date will update automatically to today’s date, which will set the recurring task to “Done”.

5.1.2. Writing a Journal Paper, Conference Paper, or White Paper

Overview :

The most common output of the researcher is a research paper - either journal paper  or conference paper . There are several main schools of thought. One is to write a short conference paper first, and then follow up by a journal paper. At the times of pandemic, conferences became less valuable, and the other school of thought became more common: start the process of writing journal papers as soon as you start working on the group and convert your writing to conference papers occasionally, in special cases, for example, for a high-value conference in computer science.

There are many great benefits in writing journal papers before completing your research tasks: it provides you with better understanding of the field, connects you to previous manuscripts developed in the lab (so that you can build on previous work rather than repeat the same basic steps), develops text for scholarship applications and grant proposals, and helps charting the direction of experimental and theoretical research efforts.

A separate type of paper is white paper. A white paper  is a short report (usually often 2-5 pages long) that introduces a problem, and then presents a solution to educate readers. They often contain well-formatted, cross-referenced figures and tables, equations, and other unique sets of data. White papers are typically used to pitch projects to potential collaborators and investors.

The examples of papers will be provided after you start the writing process.

  1. Set up a new Quest for your paper.

5.2.2. ECoS: 'Efficiency, Content, and Style' Tool

Overview:

  1. ECoS stands for 'Efficiency, Content, and Style'. ECoS is our lab's style guide. We use ECoS to help our members become better writers. It divides technical writing into types of manuscripts (called "Building Blocks'', e.g., proposals, business plans), and then subdivides each of these categories into the following three parts:         
  • The efficiency   of the piece. Efficiency refers to quickly creating meaningful text. Examples: Using templates, minimizing repetition, and using automation features (e.g., cross-referencing figures, tables, and equations).
  • The content  of the piece. Content refers to strategies and best practices for constructing actual, strong, persuasive, fundable text, ensuring the text comprehensively meets the expectations of the target audience. Example: Writing an abstract that passes the five fundamental rules, which is explained further in the YBR process.
  • The style  of the piece. Style refers to the visual appearance of the manuscript. Example: ensuring that fonts in a figure are not too small to read, and that graphical elements look polished, professional, and presentable.

How to Edit ECoS :

Part 1) Understanding How it Works

Before you edit ECoS, understand the philosophy behind it. ECoS is like a checklist–it is a resource to help people learn how to write. Level 4 headings are essentially recommended actions.

Examples of an ECoS item for designing strong figures might be:

  • Clearly label all parts of interest in photos
  • Captions  use clear and understandable language.
  • Consistently capitalize only the first word of all figure captions.

Notice how the recommendations are short and concise ideas. All recommendations are sorted into three categories: (1) strategies for efficient writing, (2) strategies for writing impactful content , and (3) strategies for designing stylistic content   that looks good. As such, everything you add to ECoS should be through this ECoS framework. You must frame the suggestion as a way to either make writing faster (“efficiency”), more impactful (“content”), or look better (“style”).

All suggestions are subdivided, as follows: (1) an explanation of the suggestion (e.g., how it works), (2) an example of incorrect or problematic behavior, (3) an example of correct or better behavior, and (4) additional resources (if applicable).

Study the following example of an ECoS Entry below:

EXAMPLE ECoS ENTRY

5.2.2.1. 1.B.2.E. Add references of scale to figures to avoid the ambiguity of size.

Explanation:

Some figures need to convey a sense of size or magnitude to the reader. Consider a figure that wants to demonstrate that a prototype is wearable. The reader might ask, How wearable is it? Is it gigantic and bulky? Is it small and comfortable?  Add a reference point inside the figure  so that the reader has a better understanding of the story, purpose, or argument behind your figure. For example, if you have a wearable prototype, add part or all of a human’s body as a point of reference.

There are many ways to capture the sense of size and scale:

A) You could use a common reference object (like a coin or fruit).

B) You could add a human body or a human hand.

C) You could use an increment scale like “1 inch = 1 mile”.

D) You could add a ruler.

Example (Incorrect):

The caption of Figure 1 is trying to claim that the prototype is comfortable to wear. But, is it…? Based on the picture, there is no way to know. In fact, the prototype looks kind of sharp and uncomfortable… it gives the reader the wrong impression.

7.1.1. Writing an Executive Summary

An executive summary is short. It’s part of a larger document and, as the name implies, summarizes the longer report.

It's a critical document that can be challenging to write. It’s more difficult to write than, say, a request for proposal (RFP), which is a straightforward description of your company’s history, product or service,   schedule of implementation  and support.

The pressure of writing an executive summary comes from the fact that everyone will pay attention to it, as it sits at the top of that heap of documents. It explains all that follows and can make or break your proposal or report. The executive summary must know the needs of the potential client and zero in on them like a laser. Fortunately, we’ll show you how to write and format your executive summary to do just that.

What is an executive summary?

An executive summary is a short document or section of a larger business or proposal. It’s used to give a reader a quick overview of the larger body of material that follows. In other words, it summarizes a report so that executives don’t have to read the whole report to understand its purpose.

It contains a short statement that addresses the problem or proposal detailed in the attached documents, and features background information, a concise analysis and a conclusion. An executive summary is designed to help executives decide whether to go forth with the proposal or not, making it critically important.

Unlike an abstract, which is a short overview, an executive summary is a condensed form of the documents contained in the proposal. Abstracts are more commonly used in academic and research-oriented writing, and act as a teaser for the reader to see if they want to read on.

What goes into an executive summary?

When writing an executive summary there are guidelines to make sure you hit all the bases. According to the many books that have been written about executive summaries, as well as training courses, seminars and professional speakers, the agreed upon length should be about five to 10 percent of the length of the whole report.

Appropriate Language

The language used should be appropriate for the target audience. One of the most important things to know before you write professionally is to understand who you are addressing. If you’re writing for a group of engineers, the language you’ll use will differ greatly from how you would write to a group of financiers. That includes more than just the words, but the content and depth of explanation.

That said, regardless of the target of your executive summary, you want to avoid being long-winded. Keep your paragraphs short and concise. A block of text, no matter how elegant or engaging, can be off-putting. Remember, it’s a summary, and people will be reading it in order to quickly and easily pull out the main items. In short : Simple + concise language getting the point across quickly.

Pithy introduction

You also want to capture a reader’s attention immediately in the opening paragraph. Just like a speech often opens with a joke to break the tension and put people at ease to better hear what’s coming, so a strong introductory paragraph can pull a reader in and make them want to read on. That doesn’t mean you start with a joke. Jokes are hard. Stick to your strengths, but remember, most readers only give you a few sentences to win them over before they move on.

Don’t forget to explain who you are as an organization and why you have the skills, personnel and experience to solve the problem raised in the proposal. This doesn’t have to be a lengthy biography, often just your name, address and contact information will do, though you’ll also want to highlight your strengths as they pertain to the proposal.

It might sound redundant, but begin the summary with a very short, bullet-pointed list of what’s to follow, like a table of contents. Then, as you flesh out those points in the executive summary, be sure to write about the documents in the same order as they appear.

Relevant information

The executive summary should not stray from the material that follows it. It’s a summary, not a place to bring up new ideas. To do so would be confusing and would jeopardize your whole proposal.

Establish the need or the problem, and convince the target audience that it must be solved. Once that is set up, then it’s important to recommend the solution and show what the value is. Be clear and firm in your recommendation.

Justify your cause. Be sure to note the key reasons why your organization is the perfect fit for the solution you’re proposing. This is the point where you differentiate yourself from competitors, be that due to methodology, testimonial from satisfied clients or whatever else you offer that is unique. But don’t make this too much about you. Be sure to keep the name of the potential client at the forefront.

Don’t neglect a strong conclusion, where you can wrap things up and once more highlight the main points.

Executive summary format

So, to put all of that information together, here’s the basic format of an executive summary:

  1. Introduction, be sure to know your audience
  2.  ( Eg. financiers, advisors, consultants, owners etc)
  3. Table of contents in the form of a bulleted list
  4. Explain the company’s role and identify strengths
  5. Explain the need, or the problem, and its importance
  6. Recommend a solution and explain its value
  7. Justify said solution by explaining how it fits the organization
  8. Strong conclusion that once more wraps up the importance of the project
  9. It is recommended to do all the above 1-8 in a short and concise manner which wouldn’t be too long + get the point across.

What to do after writing an executive summary

As with anything you write, you should always start with a draft. The first draft should hit all the marks addressed above, but don’t get yourself bogged down in making the prose perfect. Think of the first draft as an exploratory mission. You’re gathering all the pertinent information.

Next you want to thoroughly review the document to ensure that nothing important has been left out or missed. Make sure the focus is sharp and clear, and that it speaks directly to your potential client’s needs.

Proofread for style and grammar

But don’t neglect the writing. Be sure that you’re not repeating words, falling into cliché or other hallmarks of bad writing. No, you’re not seeking the Pulitzer Prize in Literature, but you also don’t want to bore the reader to the point that they miss the reason why you’re the organization that can help them succeed.

You’ve checked the content and the prose, but don’t forget style. You want to write in a way that is natural and not overly formal, but one that speaks in the manner of your target audience. If they’re a conservative firm, well then, maybe formality is called for. But more and more modern companies have a casual corporate culture, and formal writing could mistakenly cause them to think of you as old and outdated.

The last run should be proofing the copy. That means double-checking to make sure that spelling is correct, and there are no typos or grammatical mistakes. Whoever wrote the executive summary is not the best person to edit it, however. They can easily gloss over errors because of their familiarity with the work. Find someone who excels at copy-editing. If you deliver sloppy content it shows a lack of professionalism that will surely color how a reader thinks of your company.

Criticism of executive summaries

Every good argument needs a rebuttal, and while we’re advocating for the proper use of an executive summary, it would be neglectful to avoid mentioning some critiques. The most common is that an executive summary by design is too simple to capture the complexity of a large and complicated project.

It’s true that many executives might only read the summary, and in so doing, miss the nuance of the proposal. That is a risk. But if the executive summary follows the guidelines stated above, it should give a full picture of the proposal and create interest for the reader to delve deeper into the documents to get the details.

Executive summaries can vary widely in quality. Poorly written ones may lack focus, fail to highlight results, or present vague, uninspiring arguments. But if you follow best practices, your responsibility ends there—the rest is up to the reader.

If the potential candidate only reads the executive summary and isn’t interested in the details outlined in the accompanying documents, is this an organization that you want to work with? Probably not. A company that’s not as thorough and dedicated to the group as you are is only going to cause problems over the course of its completion. You always have the option of refusing their acceptance if you feel that the partnership is going to take more than it will give.

7.2. Intellectual Property Protection (Patents etc)

You’ve created something. Now what?

When students create original tools, software, hardware, media, or branding as part of a project, it’s important to understand the basics of intellectual property (IP) and when to act on it.

7.2.1. What Is IP?

Intellectual property refers to the legal rights that protect your creations. There are three common types:

  • Copyrights protect original works created (like code, UI design, text, or documents).
  • Trademarks protect brand names, logos, and identifiers that represent your project.
  • Patents protect inventions or systems, like unique technical processes or hardware.

Watch this quick video series to get familiar: Intro to Patents, Copyrights, and Trademarks

7.2.2. Do You Need IP Protection?

Most projects at the lab don’t require parents or formal IP filings. However, you should consider your options if:

  • You’re developing custom code or tools others might reuse
  • You’re designing a new system or invention
  • You’re creating media, logos, or branding for long-term use
  • You’re collaborating across labs or RSO and want to clarify the author

For example:

  • A student designed app might be open sourced under a MIT License
  • A team logo might get trademarked to protect brand identity
  • A circuit design might be patent-eligible even while open access.

If your project involves potential IP, contact CoMotion, UW’s innovation transfer office . They help student researchers and developers understand IP rights, licensing options, and commercialization paths: https://comotion.uw.edu

At minimum, document who created what, what licenses (if any) apply, and your intentions for future use. This protects your team, makes maintenance easier, and enables responsible reuse by others.

Final Thought

IP is complex, but ignoring it can lead to confusion down the road. Taking a few simple steps to understand and document your team’s work to help protect your ideas, credit your contributions, and support a culture of responsible innovation at the lab.

  • NSF lineage  – Teams must have an active NSF research award within the last five years or complete a regional I‑Corps program and receive a letter of recommendation . The national program is open only to faculty‑led teams affiliated with higher‑education institutions.
  • Executive summary and proposal  – Applicants submit an executive summary  describing team members, technology and commercial application. Selected teams interview with I‑Corps staff and, if successful, submit a full proposal.
  • Commitments  – Teams must conduct 100 customer interviews , attend all sessions, follow instructor guidance, and explore commercialization pathways. The program expects a total time commitment of 15 hours/week  (often more during kick‑off and lessons learned sessions).

7.4.4.4. Orientation (Day 0)

  • Introductions to program tools (NovoEd, Airtable Ascent, Zoom).
  • Overview of NSF I‑Corps goals and expectations.
  • Separate workshops for Entrepreneurial Leads, Technical Leads and Industry Mentors.

7.4.4.5. Kick‑Off Sessions (Days 1–3)

  • Session 1 (Kick‑Off Day 1)  – Team presentations (3‑minute pitches) followed by lectures on the Business Model Canvas and customer discovery best practices. The afternoon is dedicated to interviews with potential customers , with a mandatory office‑hour meeting that evening.
  • Session 2 (Kick‑Off Day 2)  – Team report‑outs (10 min per team) and a workshop on value propositions .
  • Session 3 (Kick‑Off Day 3)  – Team report‑outs and a workshop on customer segments .

7.4.4.6. Weekly Web Sessions (Weeks 2–6)

  • Teams are assigned to either Block A  or Block B . Each week they present updates in a 10‑minute report‑out, receive instructor feedback, and learn a new business‑model component (e.g., channels, revenue streams, key activities).
  • Office hours are offered for additional guidance.

7.4.4.7. Lessons Learned Workshop (Week 7)

  • Teams meet one‑on‑one with instructors to discuss key learnings, decide a “Go/No‑Go”  path and plan next steps.
  • Participants attend presentations on communicating I‑Corps learning and SBIR funding.

7.4.4.8. Closing Session (Final Day)

  • Each team delivers a final “Lessons Learned” presentation  and shows a short video summarizing their journey. Presentations are followed by teaching‑team comments and a closing ceremony.

7.4.4.9. Deliverables

  1. 100 Interview Summaries  – Logged in Airtable Ascent; include customer archetype, hypotheses tested and key insights.
  2. Session Slide Decks  – Standard template includes team profile, lessons learned (hypotheses, experiments, results, iteration), current Business Model Canvas and discovery plan. Additional session‑specific slides (e.g., market size estimates) may be required.
  3. Lessons Learned Video and Presentation  – Summarizes the team’s overall experience and next steps.

7.4.5. Regional I‑Corps Program

7.4.5.1. Purpose and Scope

  • Gateway to the national program  – Regional I‑Corps courses (also called I‑Corps Hubs) provide shorter, free training for STEM researchers and early‑stage entrepreneurs  to explore the market potential of their innovation. Completing a regional course and obtaining a letter of recommendation enables teams without NSF lineage to apply to the national program.
  • Experiential focus  – Like the national program, regional courses emphasize customer discovery  and use the Business Model/Lean Canvas frameworks. The goal is to help teams decide whether to pursue commercialization

7.4.5.2. Structure and Format

  • Duration  – Most Hubs offer a month‑long program  (approximately four to five weeks) with six Zoom‑based classes  and one or two instructor check‑ins.
  • Workload  – Teams spend eight to ten hours per week  completing assignments and conducting interviews. Some regional programs require 10–15 hours/week  and warn that those unable to commit should not apply.
  • Interviews  – Teams conduct ~30 customer interviews  during the course. For example, the Interior Northeast Hub’s regional course teaches teams to talk to 30 potential customers , while the Mid‑Atlantic Hub requires at least 20 interviews  and 10–15 hours/week.
  • Course components  – Regional programs typically include:
  • Kick‑off orientation and introduction to customer discovery.
  • Weekly classes covering target customer segments, value propositions and minimum viable product design.
  • Coaching sessions and office hours with instructors.
  • Final presentation summarizing learnings and market opportunity.

7.4.5.3. Eligibility and Team Composition

  • Open to broad applicants  – Regional I‑Corps is open to university‑based researchers, federal labs and community entrepreneurs; participants do not  need prior NSF funding.
  • Team size  – Regional teams often consist of 1–3 members . In contrast, the national program requires at least three members (EL, TL and IM).
  • Technology readiness  – Applicants should have an early‑stage technology  beyond the idea stage (prototype or experimental validation). Some Hubs prefer applicants with university‑affiliated technology and ask for 30 interview commitments.

7.4.5.4. Curriculum and Learning Outcomes

  • Evaluate commercialization potential  – Teams learn to assess whether there is a market need for their technology.
  • Customer segments and problem identification  – Participants practice identifying target customer segments, generating hypotheses about customer jobs, pains and gains, and crafting interview questions.
  • Interview skills  – Teams learn to conduct discovery interviews to identify why  customers experience specific problems and what solutions they currently use.
  • Entrepreneurial mindset  – The program encourages participants to embrace uncertainty, take feedback and iterate quickly.
  • Final deliverables  – A report‑out summarizing interviews, updated Lean Canvas and a plan for next steps (e.g., applying to the national program or pursuing other entrepreneurial support).

7.4.7. Customer Discovery and Interview Techniques

7.4.7.1. Principles

  • Problem‑centric questions  – Avoid pitching your product in interviews. Instead, ask customers about their roles, processes and pain points . Sample opening prompts include: “Tell me your title and what you do at your job,” “Tell me about your process for ___,” and “Do you have any problems with ___?” When discussing issues, explore why  those problems occur and how they impact costs, downtime, morale and productivity. Use open‑ended questions to encourage storytelling and dig deeper.
  • Hypothesis‑driven approach  – Before interviews, formulate hypotheses about customer jobs, pains and gains. Design questions to test those hypotheses and be prepared to pivot based on what you learn.
  • Associated costs and impacts  – Ask about energy or operational costs , downtime , safety or compliance implications  and any alternative solutions  the customer has tried. Understanding costs helps you quantify the value proposition.
  • Referrals  – Always ask whether interviewees can introduce you to additional contacts. The network effect is critical for reaching 100 interviews in the national program.

7.4.7.2. Interview Logistics

  • Scheduling  – Use tools like Calendly to arrange interviews. Schedule 7+ interviews  for each day of the Kick‑Off and maintain momentum (≈15–17 interviews per week for the national program).
  • Recording insights  – After each interview, capture notes under the headings Hypotheses, Experiments, Results and Iterate . Summarize key insights and update your Business Model Canvas accordingly.
  • Respect confidentiality  – Most interviews do not require NDAs. Discuss problems at a high level and avoid revealing proprietary information.

7.4.7.3. Business Model & Lean Canvas Work

  • Segment definition  – Identify distinct customer segments (users, buyers, payers, influencers, regulators, saboteurs and competitors). Describe their roles, characteristics and pains. Use the Lean Canvas to capture key metrics, unfair advantages and revenue streams.
  • Hypotheses testing  – For each segment, list job hypotheses, pain hypotheses and gain hypotheses. Prepare interview questions to test them.
  • Value proposition  – Synthesize a one‑sentence value proposition: “ Our [solution] helps [customer segment] who want to [job to be done] by [reducing a customer pain] and [increasing a customer gain], unlike [existing alternatives] .”
  • Storytelling and pitching  – Develop a 7‑sentence story  and a one‑minute pitch  that use ethos, pathos and logos to persuade stakeholders. Practice your 4‑minute and 10‑minute presentations ahead of lessons learned and closing sessions.

10. Frequently Asked Questions

10.1. FAQ: Why Is My YBR Gold Delta Zero?

(And why Kanban tasks don’t change your YBR)

10.1.1. Q1. Why is my YBR Gold Delta zero?

Your YBR Gold Delta is zero because you have not advanced along the YBR (Yellow Brick Road) writing process in the last evaluation period.

Even if you completed many Kanban tasks, your YBR will not change unless you complete YBR‑specific writing steps such as:

  • Stage advancement inside the YBR interface

If nothing inside the YBR changed, then:

YBR Gold Delta = 0

This is expected and correct.

10.1.2. Q2. But I worked on my project. Why doesn’t the system count that?

Because the system distinguishes between project work and manuscript/proposal progress.

  • Kanban tracks general tasks.
  • YBR tracks structured writing progress.

Kanban work may help your project overall, but it is not part of the formal writing workflow that YBR measures.

So:

Doing Kanban work ≠ advancing YBR writing milestones.

10.1.3. Q3. Why doesn’t Kanban activity change my YBR score?

10.1.3.1. YBR Gold measures progress on a formal writing path, not “how busy you are.”

If Kanban affected YBR, students could raise their YBR score by:

  • Fixing small bugs
  • Sending emails
  • Doing cleanup tasks
  • Making low‑impact edits

None of these prove real progress toward a manuscript or proposal.

To keep YBR meaningful, only writing‑related steps count.

10.1.3.2. It preserves fairness.

Consider two students:

  • Student A completes 30 Kanban chores but no draft.
  • Student B completes an outline and a full draft in YBR.

If Kanban awarded YBR Gold:

  • Student A might appear ahead  despite doing no writing.
  • Student B’s major writing progress would not be recognized.

That would make the metric misleading and unfair.

10.1.3.3. The system needs a clean signal for academic progress.

Kanban = unstructured project tasks
YBR = structured academic writing pathway

Mixing the two makes YBR impossible to interpret.

10.1.4. Q4. What should I do if I want my YBR Gold Delta to increase?

Advance your manuscript or proposal by completing YBR stages, not Kanban tasks.

Ask yourself:

“Did I meaningfully advance my writing in the YBR system this week?”

If yes → Delta will grow.
If no → Delta stays at 0.

10.1.5. Q5. Quick mental model

  • YBR = Main storyline (progress measured)
  • Kanban = Side quests (helpful, but not counted toward storyline progress)

Side quests don’t unlock new chapters.
Main story actions do.
